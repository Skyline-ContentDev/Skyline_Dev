Validate, adjust profiles, and escalate unauthorized access point threats

  • Rogue Detection: Automated identification of unauthorized access points

  • Containment Actions: Automated or manual rogue neutralization

1

Initial Rogue Assessment

⏱ 2-4 minutes

Accessing Rogue Detection:

  1. Navigate to Assurance > Dashboards > Rogue and aWIPS
  2. Review the Overview dashboard for key metrics:
    • Total Rogue Threats
    • Total AWIPS Threats
    • Total Unique Rogue Clients
    • Rogues Contained
  3. Examine the Active High Threats and Top Locations Affected for additional details
  4. Examine the High Threats over time for key identifiers
  5. Examine the Threats by Location to allocate human resources to rogue access points and any threats they may be creating
  6. Navigate to the Threats in the Rogue and aWIPS dashboard

Threat Classification Levels:

🔴 High Threats

  • Honeypot, Rogue on Wire, AP Impersonation
  • Beacon DS Attack, Beacon Wrong Channel
  • All aWIPS threats

🟡 Potential Threats

  • Interferer, Allowed Vendor (Potential)

🔵 Informational

  • Neighbor, Friendly, Allowed Vendor (Informational)
  • Allowed List

💡 Quick Risk Assessment Indicators

  • Signal Strength: Stronger signals indicate closer proximity
  • SSID Name: Look for corporate SSID spoofing attempts
  • Security Type: Open networks pose higher security risk
  • Client Count: Rogues with connected clients are active threats
2

Detailed Rogue Analysis

⏱ 5-8 minutes

Rogue Details Investigation:

  1. Click on the specific rogue AP from the list
  2. Review Threats page information:
    • MAC Address (selecting opens detailed page about that AP)
    • Detecting AP (the AP that has radio visibility to the potential rogue)
    • RSSI (the signal strength of the potential rogue)
    • SSID (any seen SSID information)
    • Containment Status (the status of containment of the rogue AP)

Understanding Threat Types:

Beacon Wrong Channel: Beacon wrong channel is when a rogue AP spoofs the beacon packets on a channel that is different from the advertised channel on the beacon frame of the managed AP. This is a type of AP impersonation attack.
Beacon DS Attack: A rogue AP that impersonates the Beacon Frame also. Beacon Frames are used to advertise the connection characteristics to all the Clients in its vicinity.
AP Impersonation: AP Impersonation is when a Rogue AP is trying to spoof or impersonate a managed AP in Catalyst Center. The Rogue AP assumes the same BSSID and ESSID of a valid AP.
Rogue on Wire: Rogue on Wire APs are connected to the wired network and are broadcasting wireless SSIDs. These APs are identified using the vendor-specific algorithm or client connections seen on the Switch Port of bridge devices.
Honeypot: Honeypot APs broadcast the same wireless SSID that is configured on any of the wireless controllers managed by Catalyst Center.
Interferer: Interferers are those APs with a signal strength greater than -75dBm and not broadcasting any corporate SSIDs.

⚡ Security Assessment Steps

  • In the Threats Dashboard, look for high level threats and address those first
  • Validate the Containment status
  • If APs are listed in the Allowed list, they will not appear on the Threats dashboard
  • If APs are not managed by a WLC in your environment, but are not a threat, they can be reclassified as Allowed: MAC Address > Actions > Add to Allowed List

💡 AP Selection Details

  • The AP listed in threats if selected reveals location information for the AP as seen by a managed AP
  • This pop out screen also shows whether or not any clients are connected to the AP
  • This would be especially risky if the client belongs to the protected environment
